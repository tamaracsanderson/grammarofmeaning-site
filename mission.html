Grammar of Meaning

field notes on how meaning gets made

Meaning isn't a thing you find; it's made — a verb, in the meeting between a person and the world (Plato's metaxy, the in-between) — which is how we hold what's too much: grief, a self that won't stay still, dying. This page is about that capacity: what meaning-making actually does for a person, why it's in trouble, and why the answer we're building is depth without dogma rather than a new doctrine.

The question

How does meaning get made? Not the answers traditions give — the moves underneath them.

The claim

Meaning isn't a thing you find or possess; it's made, a verb, in the meeting between a person and the world (the metaxy). So you study what traditions do, not what they believe.

Why it matters

Meaning-making is a capacity — one we can measure, and one we can lose. When it fails, clinicians have names for it; when it collapses across a population, the result is lethal. What's in crisis isn't the human ability to make meaning — it's the shared architecture that used to hold it.

The method

We saw it top-down first: moves that looked like the anatomy of a transformation. Now we rebuild bottom-up, each tradition in its own vocabulary, so the patterns come from the traditions — not from an AI's priors or the Western canon. Convergence only counts if it emerges.

The finding — what we test

Which of those moves recur across traditions — the same move in a different voice, an analogy you can carry — and which belong to one alone. Not "all religions say the same thing"; a testable question about what's portable.

The form

The essays, one pattern at a time; and the Garden, an interactive map of the moves — where the exploring is itself a kind of formation.

The lineage

Open-methodology comparative inquiry — corpus, coding, and tooling all published — in the lineage of Brand · Diderot · Alexander · Meadows · Smil. Spirituality is the domain; the method is the contribution.

What we mean by a rhyme

It's not perennialism (everyone secretly says the same thing) and not particularism (every tradition is an island). It's the moves: a move lets you see both what's particular to a tradition and where it rhymes with others. The rhyme is an analogy — the same gesture in a different voice (the Islamic God is not the Christian God, but devotion to the sacred is a move you can hold up against both) — and that analogy is something you can carry into your own life. The rhyme is the structure; the divergence is where each tradition's specific wisdom lives. It's basically all about the analogy.

What meaning-making does

Underneath the philosophy, meaning-making is a real human capacity — something we can watch people do, and watch some people lose. Viktor Frankl, who came out of the camps, called it the will to meaning: when a person can answer why, they can bear almost any how. A meaning-system lets people endure suffering, steady their emotions, and look at their own mortality without flinching away. It isn't decoration on a life; it's load-bearing.

The moves of coping

You can see the capacity working most clearly when it's under strain. When an event violates someone's meaning-system — the diagnosis, the loss, the betrayal that "wasn't supposed to happen" — there are two moves available (Park & Folkman map this precisely). Assimilation: bend the event to fit the beliefs you already hold — "this illness is a test of faith." Accommodation: revise the beliefs to fit the event — letting the world re-teach you what you thought you knew. These are moves in exactly the sense this project means — the same kind of gesture we catalog across traditions, here turned inward on a single life.

The capacity matures

And it grows. The developmental arc (Robert Kegan's self-transforming mind) runs toward being able to hold more than one meaning-world at once — to stand inside several frameworks, even contradictory ones, without feeling that another person's meaning is a threat to your own. That capacity is the whole rationale for the Garden, and for pluralism. The Garden isn't a neutral comparison chart; it's a place to practice exactly this — moving between meaning-worlds until the moving gets easier.

Why it matters — and what its absence costs

Meaning earns its keep most where nothing can be fixed. In low-control situations — illness, grief, the irreversible — it's meaning, not problem-solving, that regulates emotion. It also binds people to one another: shared meaning is what holds a family or a community together, the prosocial good. And it quietly buffers the fear of death. Its absence is just as concrete. Clinicians have names for the hole it leaves: demoralization; spiritual distress, a condition nurses actually chart at the bedside; complicated grief that won't resolve. At population scale the absence turns lethal — what Case & Deaton named deaths of despair. The capacity is real because you can measure what happens when it's gone.

The gap — and our answer to it

As inherited religious frameworks recede — the rise of the nones — more people are left without a ready-made meaning-home. The usual responses are to convert someone into a tradition, or to hand them a thin secular substitute. This project does neither. The answer is to let people engage the world's meaning-making traditions — religious ones very much included — through analogy and rhyme, with no pressure to accept a metaphysical claim. You might come to accept one; you don't have to. That's what depth without dogma means.

Take Rabiʿa al-ʿAdawiyya, the Sufi mystic, who prayed to love God for God's own sake — not for the promise of paradise or the fear of hell. Her devotion is thoroughly religious. But the move — loving the beloved for its own sake, not for the reward it pays out — is portable. You can hold it up against a Christian's caritas, a parent's love, a craftsman's devotion to the work. You don't have to share her God to carry her move.

Why it works — third things

Parker Palmer has a name for how this happens: a third thing. The soul, he says, is shy — it won't be stared at directly. But set a poem, a story, a song between you and it, and you can approach the hardest material sidelong, through the third thing, without flinching away. You can't look straight at your own grief; you can look at Rabiʿa's, or Zhuangzi's, or a song someone else wrote about a breakup — and find your own in it. The same artifact is two things at once: direct meaning-making for the person who made it, and a third thing for everyone who later meets it. Every tradition's text was someone's direct reckoning; for us it becomes the third thing we make our own meaning through, by analogy. The essays and the Garden are built to be exactly that — third things to think with.

Four needs a meaning makes
PurposeA sense of direction — connecting today's actions to a desired future. Without it, a life drifts.
ValueA moral framework — a basis for right and wrong, and for justifying what one does.
EfficacyA sense of agency — the belief that you can actually make a difference.
Self-worthIdentity and belonging — a sense of one's own positive value, and a place among others.
 Roy Baumeister's four needs. A life can run on intense purpose and still fracture if it lacks value or efficacy — the four work together, not one at a time.
Why build it

The contemporary crisis isn't an evaporation of human capability — it's the collapse of a shared cultural architecture, leaving each person to author meaning in isolation.

So the Garden is a rebuilt commons for meaning-making — a record of how humans have made meaning, for a moment when the inherited commons has thinned. Not wellness, not one tradition: a commons. It gives the materials to the seeker who builds meaning deliberately; the stakes are the unmoored, who can't do it alone.
