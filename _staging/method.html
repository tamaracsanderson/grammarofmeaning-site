Grammar of Meaning · about the method

How the reading is done

The promise of the project is its method: read each tradition in its own words, from the ground up, and let any pattern have to earn its way in. Here's how that works — and how it's kept honest.

The move: top-down, then bottom-up

The work began top-down — noticing recurring "moves" across traditions that looked like the anatomy of a transformation. That's a starting hypothesis, not a finding. So the real work runs the other way: rebuild it bottom-up, coding each tradition in its own vocabulary first, before any cross-tradition comparison is allowed. A Buddhist passage is read in Buddhist terms; a Christian one in Christian terms. Cross-tradition equivalence comes later, from aggregation — or not at all.

This is the discipline that keeps the answer from being smuggled into the question. If the patterns are real, they surface from the traditions themselves — not from an AI's priors, and not from the Western canon doing the sorting.

The reading, step by step

1
Gather, everywhere at once. Sources are hunted across many archives in parallel — a tradition's home-region scholarship alongside the Western indexes — so coverage isn't quietly biased toward whatever got digitized first.
2
Keep the receipts — including the misses. Every source enters with where it came from; empty-handed searches are logged too. Where coverage is thin, you can see it, instead of it hiding as a silent gap.
3
Break texts into quotable passages. A book or recording is a container; the passage is the unit of analysis. Container-counts aren't depth.
4
Label each passage in the tradition's own words first. No imported outside categories at this stage — the tradition's vocabulary leads.
5
Only then, look across. Where independently-arising moves line up between traditions, that's a candidate rhyme — to be tested, not assumed.

The reliability panel — instead of one reader

A solo researcher has no room full of human coders. The substitute: each judgment is put to a panel of independent model-cohorts, reading from three different chairs, and their agreement is scored. Disagreement isn't smoothed over — it's the signal that a call is shaky.

The insiderreads as a member of the tradition — does this honor how it understands itself?
The outsiderreads as a comparativist — what does this look like from outside?
The practitionerreads as someone who does the practice — what does it ask of a life?

What would prove it wrong

A pattern only counts if it emerges from the bottom-up coding — so the honest test is built in: when moves converge, ask whether they converge because the corpus leans heavily on a few related traditions, or whether they hold up across traditions with very different corpus weights. A rhyme that's just an artifact of what we happened to collect is not a finding.

Bias, made visible — not pre-filtered. The corpus skews toward traditions with long text-production and digitization histories. Rather than hide that behind "principled selection," the skew is documented and tracked, so a reader can weigh it. A bias you can see is a bias you can correct for; a bias you pruned away is one you've only hidden.

The lineage — the category this belongs to

Open-methodology comparative inquiry: the corpus, the coding, and the tooling are all published, so others can extend, audit, or contest the work. The forebears each lend one move:

Stewart Brand
share the how — tools and access, not just the conclusions.
Diderot
the encyclopedic ambition — comprehensive, with provenance.
Christopher Alexander
patterns across contexts — but derived empirically, not from intuition alone.
Donella Meadows
archetypes that recur across domains — here, the inner life.
Vaclav Smil
vast empirical breadth — but with the method published, not withheld.

Spirituality is the proof-of-concept domain; the method is the contribution, and it's domain-agnostic.
