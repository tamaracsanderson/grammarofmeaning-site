The reading room

his own early writing, read in full · one measure of ~66ch

The Message of the Spirituals

Howard Thurman · 1928 · public domain (published pre-1931)

The songs the enslaved sang were never only songs. They were a way of keeping the interior life intact when every outward circumstance conspired to break it — a language spoken beneath the language the master could hear, carrying grief and hope in the same breath, so that the singing itself became an act of survival.

To listen closely is to discover a people who refused to let the fact of their bondage become the final fact about them. The spiritual does not deny the trouble; it names the trouble plainly, and then it reaches past it, toward a homeland the body had never seen and the spirit somehow already knew.

Here, then, is the quiet insistence at the center of the tradition: that no condition, however cruel, can reach the place where a person meets what is genuine in themselves. The song guards that place. It keeps the door open.

[full public-domain text — transcribed from the Howard Thurman Papers Project]
