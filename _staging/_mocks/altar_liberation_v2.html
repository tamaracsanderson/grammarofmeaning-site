Liberation — a few more

New options in the merged register, from your own ideas: the open birdcage · the redrawn broken chains · open hands offering a heart · open hands releasing a bird.

1The open birdcage

The door swung wide, the songbird gone up and out. The empty cage carries the read — release from bondage, not a spirit descending.

Recommended

2The broken chains, redrawn

Chunky links with a real snap in the middle — the two halves pulling apart, sparks at the break. Heavy strokes make the boldest silhouette of the four.

3Open hands + a heart

Two open, upturned palms releasing a heart that rises from them. Generous and releasing — the giving-away, not the grip.

4Open hands + a bird

The same open palms, now letting a small songbird take flight. Freedom given wings — a bird kept thin and forked so it never reads as the descending dove.

Recommended: the broken chains, redrawn (option 2). It survives shrinking to 32px better than any of the others — the chunky links plus the clean snap give the strongest, most instantly-legible silhouette, which is what an altar-icon most needs. The open birdcage (1) is the most poetic and the most clearly not Grace; open-hands-with-a-heart (3) is the warmest but softest at small sizes. If you want the human body in the frame, pick 1 or 3 over the two bird options only where the bird risks reading dove-ish at a glance.

On this altar This is the Christian altar only. These Liberation forms — cage, chains, open hands — are drawn in the house language, with the devotional nod kept sparing: a gilded ground-disc behind each icon and a single liturgical-red accent. The merger rule is fixed: one common house language (warm ground, green line, hairline rules, house type) plus a per-tradition nod (each tradition later gets its own sacred forms and its own accent, on the same house ground). Schematic and provisional — the weighting shown is illustrative, not yet coded.
