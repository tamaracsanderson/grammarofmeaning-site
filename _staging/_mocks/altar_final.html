A Christian altar — the seven weights

Seven ways this one tradition has answered a single question — how does a person get saved? — each drawn in the house language with a sparing devotional nod, plus one niche left empty.

Atonement

Agnus Dei · the Lamb

The one who takes on the weight — slain, yet standing in victory.

Grace

The descending dove

Gift, not earnings — the Spirit poured down before it is asked for.

Liberation

The broken chain

Bondage snapped — the captive set free.

Communion

Chalice · the open table

The meal that re-members — bread broken, cup shared, all welcomed.

Judgment

The scales

Every life weighed — the reckoning the whole altar leans toward.

Cooperation

The ladder

Grace met by effort — the long ascent, rung by rung.

Theosis

The mandorla

The self remade into light — union, not merely pardon.

The empty niche

Reserved

A weight not yet drawn — the slot the tradition keeps open.

The merger is fixed: one common house language — the gilded ground disc, moss-green linework carrying every structure, white where a form needs body. The devotional nod is sparing — gold-leaf for halos and sacred metal, one stroke of liturgical red per icon. Greens hold the frame; gold and red touch only what is most sacred.

On this altar

This is the Christian altar only. The seven forms are Christianity's own inheritance — the Lamb, the dove, the broken chain, the chalice, the scales, the ladder, the mandorla — drawn in its own accent. The merger rule stays fixed: one common house language plus a per-tradition nod. Each tradition later gets its own sacred forms on the same house ground.
