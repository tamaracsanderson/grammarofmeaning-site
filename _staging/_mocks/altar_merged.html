Every tradition sets down a handful of sacred objects and gives each one weight. Here is the Christian ledge, drawn in the house language — with a devotional nod to its own inheritance.

Section 02 · one object, four drafts

Liberation — options

The freed-from-bondage motive. Four candidate forms in the merged register — each with a foreground icon and a bold silhouette read.

1Broken chains

Bondage snapped. The plainest, boldest silhouette — reads instantly at any size.

Recommended

2The freed hands

Two hands rising, cuffs split at the wrists. Freedom plus a human body — warmer than a bare object.

3The opened manacle

A single shackle, sprung open. Quiet and precise — but reads less obviously than a break.

4The Anastasis, simplified

One hand pulling another up out of the grave-line — the icon's core gesture, stripped of its figures.

Recommended: the freed hands (option 2). It keeps the human body in the frame — the whole altar is about a person being weighed and released — without the theological freight of the full Anastasis, which you have mixed feelings on. Broken chains (1) is the safest bold silhouette if you want maximum legibility; the simplified Anastasis (4) is the most doctrinally specific but the hardest to read at 32px.

On this altar This is the Christian altar only. The devotional forms here — Lamb, dove, chalice, chains — are Christianity's own inheritance, drawn in its own accent. The merger rule is fixed: one common house language (warm ground, green line, hairline rules, house type) plus a per-tradition nod (each tradition later gets its own sacred forms and its own accent, on the same house ground). Schematic and provisional — the weighting shown is illustrative, not yet coded.
