The Living Garden

A herbarium for the human spirit — every tradition a plant, every reading a season of growth.

The identity IS the Garden: warm rag paper, luminous living greens, botanical linework, and an organic-refined serif. Traditions are drawn as specimens; the recurring "moves" of meaning-making are drawn as a growing branch. A pilgrimage through a collection that is still alive and putting out leaves.

Palette

Living greens, warm soil

Greens chosen with intent — luminous fern and clean sage against warm parchment, warmed by terracotta soil and honey. No near-black, no murky grey-green anywhere.

Typography

Fraunces & Spectral

An organic, slightly wild display serif for warmth; a calm literary serif for reading; a clean humanist sans for labels and data.

Display — Fraunces (opsz, soft/wonky serif)
How meaning gets made.
Aa Gg & Rhyme · metaxy · fern
Why Fraunces. It has the botanical-plate warmth — ink-trap curves, an old-catalogue feeling — without tipping precious. Optical sizing lets headlines breathe and small caps stay crisp. It reads like a naturalist's hand-set title page.
Body — Spectral (literary serif)
Each tradition is read in its own vocabulary. We look for the moves — the small operations of meaning-making — that recur as a rhyme from one plant to the next, across the whole garden of traditions.
Why Spectral. Designed for on-screen long-form. Generous x-height, low contrast, sits calmly under Fraunces. Reading pages stay legible at length; italics carry the tradition-terms elegantly.
Labels & Data — Inter (humanist sans)
Agent · Substrate · Operation · Outcome
Specimen no. 0114 — Family: Abrahamic — Reception: growing
Why Inter. Diagram, taxon-data, and flowchart text must be maximally legible at small sizes. Inter keeps the botanical mood serif-led while the machinery of the Method stays engineered-clean.

The Garden — Specimen Plate

A tradition, drawn as a plant

Each tradition enters the Garden as a specimen: its own name, its family, and a reception that grows over time — leaves for centuries of commentary.

Family · Abrahamic

Pauline Christianity

reads in its own vocabulary — charis, pistis, dikaiosynē

A tradition that names the human problem as estrangement and its resolution as being conformed — remade into a likeness held in advance. Its reception has grown for two millennia, each commentary a new leaf on the same stem.

The Method — A Move, Specimen-Drawn

The move as a growing branch

Our signature diagram. A "move" of meaning-making is one stem that sprouts four nodes in order — Agent → Substrate → Operation → Outcome. Read it left-to-right; legibility first, botany second.

Seed & Bloom — Agent & Outcome
Nodes — Substrate & Operation
Stem — the reading’s direction

Romans 8:29 — “those he foreknew he also predestined to be conformed to the image of his Son.” The same four-node branch drawn for a Buddhist, Stoic, or Yoruba passage lets two plants be compared node-for-node: that is the rhyme.

Meaning is not stored in the plant. It is made in the in-between — the metaxy — where a reader and a tradition meet and something takes root.

For the curious

The expandable "for the curious" pattern reads as a labeled specimen drawer — hatch-textured, honey-ruled — not a plain accordion.

For the curious
Why “rhyme” and not “the same idea”?

Two traditions rarely mean the same thing. But they often make the same move — the same operation on different substrate, toward a differently-named outcome. A rhyme, not a synonym.

Field note. Pauline “conformed to the image” and a Confucian account of becoming a junzi share a move — a self reshaped toward a pattern held in advance — while disagreeing on almost everything else. The branch diagram makes the shared move visible without flattening the difference.

For the curious
How does a specimen “grow”?

Each new reading — a commentary, a translation, a lived retelling — adds a leaf. The plate you see is a snapshot of a plant still in season.
