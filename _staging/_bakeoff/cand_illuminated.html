Augustine of Hippo

LATIN CHRISTIAN · 354–430 CE · NORTH AFRICA

In the Confessions, Augustine asks quid est ergo tempus? — “what, then, is time?” — and answers that he knows until asked. The move: interrogatio, turning a lived certainty into an open question, so that meaning is re-made in the asking rather than assumed.

✦ For the curiousWhy call it a “move,” and why a “rhyme”? ▾
The unit

A move is one operation and its arguments — the smallest thing a tradition does to make meaning, not the doctrine it teaches. We read each passage emically (in the tradition’s own words) before we ever compare, so we’re never importing a foreign category.

emic — described from inside a tradition’s own vocabulary. Its partner, etic, describes from outside, in comparative terms. The Grammar codes emic first, compares etic second.
The rhyme

When the same shape of move recurs across traditions that never met, we call it a rhyme: same gesture, different voice — like A : B :: C : D. Not proof of contact, and not a claim that all paths are one — only that the underlying grammar recurs.

This gathering is part of the design, not a footnote: the curious reader gets the same vellum, the same gold rule, the same care as the front matter.
