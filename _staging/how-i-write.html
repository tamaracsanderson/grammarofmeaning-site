How I Write

The public conversation about AI and writing tends to land in one of two bins: either a human wrote it or a machine did. My process doesn't fit either.

By Tamara Sanderson  ·  a method statement

I built the instrument these essays are written from. Before I wrote a word, I built the Library: a growing collection of primary sources from hundreds of traditions, the pipeline that acquires and classifies them, and a method that cross-checks every interpretation against multiple independent models. I'm not only the editor-in-chief of these essays. I'm the architect of the archive underneath them.

And the archive exists for a specific reason: to get past what any single mind — or any single model — already over-weights. Left to my own knowledge, I reach for the familiar: the Western, the Anglo-Protestant, the already-canonized, the names that come first because they've been repeated most. A model trained on that same skewed record over-weights exactly the same things. Building bottom-up from primary sources across hundreds of traditions is how you correct for it — my bias and the machine's both. The instrument is, before anything else, a bias-correction machine. That is what rigor means here: not writing faster, but reaching further than one mind could — to the sources themselves, checked and cited.

A necessary honesty: what I've built is the instrument, and the method for reading it. These essays draw on that archive and test their claims against it; they do not report finished cross-tradition findings. The work that would say "here is what converges across hundreds of traditions" is the frontier of the project, not a result of it yet. I'll tell you what I built and how I check. I won't tell you I've proven what I haven't.

The method is what I call authored design — the way I was trained to work at IDEO: diverge wide, iterate relentlessly, put everything through crit, then cut ruthlessly. The tool changed; the discipline didn't. Each AI session works like a different specialist in a studio — one surfaces sources, one stress-tests a claim, one drafts, one runs an editorial review — and I'm the creative director. The vision, the standards, and every final call are mine. The closest analogy isn't a lone author at a desk; it's a Renaissance workshop, or Studio Ghibli: many hands touch the work, one person is responsible for what it is, and signs it. I've built infrastructure at this depth across genres — novels, picture books — but here the whole studio serves one question: how meaning gets made across traditions.

This is also how my mind works — non-linear and divergent; I think in connections, not straight lines, and for the first time the tool works the way I do rather than the other way around. The AI doesn't replace my thinking. It matches its shape — and holds me to the version of myself that thinks carefully. The guides and review frameworks I build are accountability structures: they keep the thinker on the page, not the reactor.

More iteration produces more originality, not less. The first draft — the first structure, the first metaphor — is the borrowed one; it's whatever the well-worn paths hand you. The original version lives in the twelfth attempt, after the familiar has been tried and discarded. AI gets me to the discardable draft faster, which frees my whole attention for the work that is actually mine: whether a sentence is true, whether an example earns its place, whether the reading is faithful to the source. Neuroscientist Vivienne Ming's research suggests most people use AI to substitute for their own thinking — and lose engagement — while a small minority argue with it, demand counter-arguments, and use it to think harder than they could alone. Every layer of my process is built to keep me in that second group. That isn't cognitive outsourcing. It's cognitive architecture.

Why I disclose. Because the slop everyone rightly fears is a failure of judgment, not a failure of tools — a power saw can build a house or take off a hand, and the question was never whether you used the saw, but whether you know what you're building and whether the house stands. And because silence keeps the conversation stuck in a pure-versus-cheating binary that serves the people already comfortable in the old system — not the ones for whom a more rigorous process was the whole point.

I'm not interested in whether AI-assisted work "counts." I'm interested in making the truest work I can, being transparent about how it's made, and letting it speak for itself.
