How I write  ·  authored design

How I Write

The public conversation about AI and writing tends to land in one of two bins: either a human wrote it or a machine did. My process doesn't fit either.

By Tamara Sanderson  ·  a method statement

I use AI the way I was trained to design at IDEO — through a design thinking methodology built on divergence, iteration, and curation. The tool has changed. The discipline hasn't.

There's a second reason the process looks the way it does. I'm neurodivergent — non-linear, divergent, ADHD. The traditional writing process, the one most people picture when they hear "writing" — sit alone, draft sequentially, hold one thread, revise in order — assumes a brain that works in straight lines. Mine doesn't. It never has. I think in connections, not sequences. I work across ten dimensions simultaneously and need a system that can keep up with that without asking me to slow down, linearize, or pretend I'm processing the way the template expects.

The AI doesn't replace my thinking. It matches the shape of it. For the first time, the tool works the way my brain does — not the other way around.

I call the process authored design: designing a book the way Pixar designs a film — architecture first, prototype everything, test relentlessly, cut ruthlessly — but the final product is authored. It has a voice. It has a lens. It has my losses and my obsessions in it. The design process serves the authorship, not the other way around.

The Process

Every piece I write — essays, fiction, picture books — moves through the same stages.

Research and infrastructure. Before I write a word of final text, I build the architecture underneath it. For nonfiction, this means exploring an idea from every angle — testing framings, surfacing references, pressure-testing claims. For creative writing, this means building comprehensive guides: character psychology profiles, voice documents, world-building systems, plot architectures, editorial query frameworks. Any technology or science that appears in the work gets a full research report — McKinsey-level analysis, not casual googling — using tools like Gemini Deep Research alongside my own synthesis.

This infrastructure is not project-specific. It's the method. I've built guides at this depth across multiple projects and genres: a 480-page production bible for a dark academia literary novel, with character psychology through MBTI, Enneagram, and Big Five for every character, historical settings verified against primary sources, a bibliography of over 200 academic works, and chapter-by-chapter outlines with thematic analysis, foreshadowing tracking, and revision queries. A complete world-building system for a middle grade environmental comedy, with verified climate science, Texas energy grid data, food system research, and eco-anxiety studies — all translated into the voice of an eleven-year-old narrator. A full production bible for a literary speculative novel, with voice guides containing sentence-level examples for five narrators, a product design document detailed enough to pitch to actual venture capitalists, and thematic architecture mapped through Hofstadter, Goffman, attachment theory, and Buddhist koans. A full positioning, voice, and plot architecture for a picture book series, drafted before a single page of the books was written. A single novel might generate a thousand pages of infrastructure before a word of final text exists. This is the divergent phase. It's deliberately excessive. The goal isn't output. It's optionality and precision.

Iteration. From that infrastructure, I co-write. And rewrite. And rewrite again. A novel may go through four or more complete rewrites — not polishing passes, but full structural reimaginings of the entire book. Within each rewrite, a single chapter may go through twelve or more drafts, each testing a different architecture, different emotional beats, different decisions about what the scene is actually about. Within those drafts, individual paragraphs go through their own cycle. The AI drafts with me. I decide what survives.

What this looks like in practice: Version 1 of a chapter might be a wrestling match with three characters rushing the stage. Version 4 tries a different structure entirely — an interview format. Version 7 discovers that the real scene isn't the event itself but the audience's reaction. Version 12 finds the image that was always underneath: a gesture that spreads silently through a crowd, a piece of audio evidence played through the school AV system, a narrator realizing the chapter was never about the performance — it was about what happened after. None of those discoveries exist in the first draft. They emerge through the accumulated pressure of trying and discarding everything that isn't true.

Crit review. Every chapter gets pressure-tested the way a design prototype would in a studio crit — multiple times, from multiple angles. I run each version through a simulated review panel: a child psychologist evaluating the character development, an editor checking structure and pacing, readers who mirror the book's characters, a parent reading for emotional truth. I incorporate their feedback, then run the revised chapter through dedicated evaluation frameworks I've built for the project — editorial queries checking voice consistency, emotional arc, comedy, and stakes; specialized guides for humor, science accuracy, and character psychology. These reviews run across multiple sessions, and each one generates revisions that feed back into the next draft.

Final curation. The last stage is ruthless subtraction. Most of what was generated gets cut. What survives is what I chose — every example, every structural decision, every sentence.

What the AI Does

The AI gives me speed, scope, and memory. It holds the full context of a long argument or a complex fictional world across sessions. It can test a structural reorganization in seconds that would take me a day to draft by hand. It surfaces connections across disciplines faster than I could find them alone. It functions as an embedded research assistant within the writing flow — I can check a historical detail, compare a description against primary sources, or explore a psychological framework without breaking stride the way a separate research session would require. In creative writing, it can generate variations of a scene, help me stress-test character psychology against developmental frameworks, or hold an entire novel's worth of voice and arc guides in active memory while I revise a single chapter. It is an extraordinary thinking and prototyping tool.

What I Do

I make every editorial and intellectual decision. In nonfiction: which philosophers to engage, which examples earn their place, whether a given sentence is true. In fiction: whose voice this character has, what the story is really about underneath the plot, which draft captures the emotional truth and which is just technically competent, whether the comedy is also doing character work. The AI generates options and raw material. I generate judgment — and the entire architectural framework that guides the project.

There's a dimension of this process that's harder to explain but may be the most important: the guides I build aren't just craft tools. They're accountability structures for my own thinking. I build voice guides, revision systems, and character documents that hold me to the version of myself that thinks carefully — the one who holds both/and tensions, who questions her own frameworks, who has genuine sympathy for every position she analyzes. Under pressure, under fatigue, under the defensive reflexes that show up in any creative process, it's easy to default to a lesser version of your own mind. The infrastructure keeps me honest. It ensures that the self who shows up on the page is the thinker, not the reactor.

For this project

For grammarofmeaning, the studio goes one level deeper. Before I wrote a single essay, I built the instrument the essays draw on: a database of roughly 392,000 primary sources across some 400 traditions, the pipeline that acquires and classifies them, and a method that cross-checks every interpretation against multiple independent AI models. I'm not just the editor-in-chief of these pieces — I'm the architect of the archive underneath them. These essays are authored from a research instrument I designed and built. That's about as far from prompt-and-publish as writing gets.

Why More Iteration Means More Originality

There's an assumption that AI makes creative work more derivative. In my experience, the opposite is true. If I went with the first things in my mind — the first plot structure, the first character arc, the first metaphor — I'd be reproducing the patterns I've already absorbed from other books, common tropes, familiar frameworks. Everyone's first draft draws from the most well-worn paths. That's how pattern recognition works. The obvious answer comes first.

Anne Lamott wrote that all good writing begins with a shitty first draft — that the draft exists so you have something to revise, and the real work is always the revision. AI gets me to the shitty first draft faster. Not because speed is the point, but because the draft was never where the writing lived. The writing lives in the twelfth version, where the familiar structures have been tried and discarded and what remains is the version I couldn't have reached by going with my first instinct.

There's a cognitive reason this works. Writers talk about the generator and the revisor — two modes of thinking that compete for the same bandwidth. When you're generating, the critic in your head shuts you down mid-sentence. When you're revising, the generator keeps wanting to add more. They fight each other, and the fight slows everything down. The AI handles the generation. That frees my entire brain for the work I'm actually good at: deciding what the character is feeling, whether the joke lands, how the bodies are positioned in the scene, whether you can picture yourself standing in that room. The texture. The judgment. The choices that make a scene feel true instead of just technically competent.

The studio model also lets me pull from a wider range of sources than any single mind could hold at once — developmental psychology and Don Quixote and Texas environmental science and Kohut's self psychology, all in the same chapter, all pressure-tested against each other. That cross-pollination produces something more original than working from memory alone, not less. The first idea is usually the borrowed one. The original one lives further in.

Why This Isn't What People Picture

When most people hear "AI-assisted writing," they picture someone typing a prompt and publishing what comes back. That describes a failure of judgment, not a process. My process is the opposite of that in every dimension.

Traditional writing is linear even when it's messy. You draft, you revise, you get feedback from a few humans over weeks. The bottleneck is production — how fast you can generate and test alternatives. Most writers hold two or three versions of a scene in their head. They pick one and commit because they don't have the capacity to run twelve.

Authored design removes the production bottleneck entirely. The bottleneck moves to judgment — which is where design thinking always said it should be. Generate wide, converge ruthlessly, test against real constraints.

The closest analogy isn't a single author at a desk. It's a studio. Renaissance painters ran workshops — the master held the vision, directed every piece, painted the critical passages, and signed the work. Multiple hands touched the painting. One person was responsible for what it was. Miyazaki runs Studio Ghibli the same way: every frame passes through his judgment, but he's not drawing every frame alone. Warhol's Factory made the artist-as-director model explicit.

That's how I work. Each AI session functions like a different specialist in the studio — one builds character psychology, one tests voice consistency, one runs the editorial review, one drafts scenes, one stress-tests the science. I'm the creative director. The vision, the standards, and every final call are mine. The studio lets me work at a scale and intensity that would be impossible alone.

This isn't a model I adopted because AI made it possible. It's how I've always worked. Fifteen years in collaborative environments — IDEO, Google, WordPress, Oliver Wyman — where the product was always the result of a team, not a single hand. My first published book was co-authored. My first unpublished novel was co-written fiction. I have never once produced my best work alone at a desk. The solo-author model wasn't my natural mode. It was just the only one publishing offered. AI didn't change how I think. It gave me a studio that fits the way I've always thought.

The AI doesn't reduce my involvement. It multiplies the decisions I make — every draft is a new set of choices, every panel review generates new questions, every evaluation framework surfaces things I need to reconsider. It raises the standard I hold myself to, because it removes the excuse of "I didn't have time to try it another way."

Why I Disclose

I put a note on my work because I believe the process matters, and because the current conversation needs more people describing how they actually use these tools — honestly, without defensiveness, and without diminishing the human judgment that shapes the final product.

There's a structural reason this matters beyond personal transparency. Right now, the people who feel most permission to use AI are in the highest-paid industries — tech, finance, consulting, law. The people who feel most pressure to hide it or avoid it entirely are in the lowest-paid creative fields — writing, art, humanities — where the personal cost is already the highest and the financial return is already the lowest. A software engineer using AI to write code faster is "innovative." A novelist using AI to develop characters is "cheating." The engineer bills $300 an hour. The novelist spent three years on a book for a $15,000 advance — if she's lucky. Most manuscripts never sell at all. The rejection rate is brutal, and the traditional process — one project, linear, years of solitary work — means betting your livelihood on a single submission. The person who could most benefit from the ability to develop multiple projects, iterate faster, and diversify her creative risk is the one being told the tool is off limits.

The pattern runs deeper. The people who feel most permission within those fields are men in technical roles. The people who feel most shame are women, creatives, and people in the humanities. If that holds, these tools won't disrupt existing power structures. They'll reinforce them. The men in tech get more productive. The women in creative fields work twice as hard to prove their work is "really theirs." And the gap widens along the exact lines it's always widened along.

Underneath all of this is an older story: the starving artist as proof of authenticity. Writing has always been low-paid, high-risk, and brutally personal — and the culture has always treated that suffering as evidence that the work is real. But telling artists they must struggle alone and unaided isn't protecting art. It's protecting a system that has always undervalued it. There's a reason sustainable creative work has historically required patrons, collectives, or studios — from Renaissance workshops to Pixar. The lone genius at a desk was never the norm. It was a mythology that served the people who acquire the work, not the people who make it. Telling writers they can't use these tools is telling them to keep playing financial Russian roulette and call it integrity.

Neuroscientist Vivienne Ming's research offers a useful frame for what's actually at stake. Her research suggests that roughly 90–95% of people use AI to substitute for their own thinking — outsourcing answers, confirming existing assumptions, offloading cognitive work — and that this group shows declines in the brain activity associated with active engagement. But a small minority — she puts it at around 5% — use AI differently: they argue with it, demand counter-arguments, use it to stress-test their own reasoning rather than replace it. That group, on her account, matches or outperforms people working without AI. The predictors of being in that group aren't IQ or technical skill. They're curiosity, intellectual humility, and the willingness to sit with uncertainty.

The process I've described in this document — the infrastructure, the iteration, the panel reviews, the editorial queries — is designed to keep me in that 5%. Every layer of the system exists to prevent the substitution Ming warns about. The AI doesn't think for me. It creates the conditions for me to think harder, longer, and across more dimensions than I could alone. That's not cognitive outsourcing. It's cognitive architecture.

I disclose because silence contributes to that pattern. Every person who uses AI thoughtfully and says nothing makes it easier for the conversation to stay stuck in the binary: either you're "pure" or you're "cheating." That binary serves people who were already comfortable in the old system. It doesn't serve neurodivergent thinkers, women in male-dominated tool ecosystems, or anyone whose best work requires a process the traditional model didn't account for.

The stories about AI and writing that make the news are almost always about shortcuts: plagiarism, slop, corners cut, work not checked. Those stories are real and the concerns are legitimate. But they describe a failure of judgment, not a failure of tools. A power saw can build a house or take off a hand. The question isn't whether you used the saw. It's whether you know what you're building, whether you checked every joint, and whether the house stands up.

I'm not interested in the debate over whether AI-assisted work "counts." I'm interested in making the best work I can, being transparent about how I make it, and letting the work speak for itself.
