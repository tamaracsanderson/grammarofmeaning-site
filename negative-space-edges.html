Edges & the meta-move

A move is a node. An edge is a connecting line between moves — and the cross-text edges are exactly where the rhymes, cousins, and false friends live. This is where a single reading opens onto the whole corpus.

§1What an edge is

The grammar codes each move on its own. Edges code the relations between moves — the connecting lines. There are three families, and they widen outward: edges within a text (how its moves hold together), edges out of a text (how its unfilled gaps pass to later readers), and edges across texts (how a move here rhymes with a move there). That third family is the comparative payoff — convergence, cousinhood, and false-friendship are all edge types.

§3The full taxonomy — nine named edges, three families (candidate — pending 5IRR)

Named like the grammar, so they're memorable — and each with a coding definition (what a coder checks to draw it).

Family A — within a text (how its moves hold together)

edgewhat it connectscoding definition
THEN (production)move N → move N+1does N's outcome become N+1's substrate? (the sequence)
ANSWERS (completion)a later/other move → an earlier gapdoes move M supply a value for move N's opaque×in-focus gap? (can run backward)
NESTS (containment)a move → the meta-move that holds itis N a component whose narrator = the enclosing move's agent? (recursion)

Family B — out of a text (how gaps pass to later readers)

edgewhat it connectscoding definition
BEQUEST (reception)a residual gap → a later reader/traditionis this gap unfillable from inside the text (residual)? → it's willed to reception
FORK (schism)a residual → a new-eyes reading that retractsdoes the reception-fill SUBSTITUTE (retract the core)? → a fork / divergence (Augustine vs. Arminius)

Family C — across texts (the corpus — where Q2 convergence lives)

edgewhat it connectscoding definition
RHYMEa move here :: a move there (A:B::C:D)same operation + same frame-role shape, different agent/sitz/voice → real convergence
COUSINa near-rhymerhymes on operation but differs on type or outcome — a family resemblance, not identity
FALSE-FRIENDsame surface, different movesame words/motif, DIFFERENT operation — the convergence trap; flag so it is NOT counted as convergence
COUNTERa deliberate oppositesame agent/voice/substrate, opposite valence (love ↔ hate) — a contrary across texts
Why Family C is the thesis. The whole comparative question — which observations converge across traditions that share no historical contact? — is a claim about RHYME edges. And the discipline that keeps it honest is the FALSE-FRIEND edge: two moves can wear the same vocabulary and do different work, and counting those as convergence is exactly the flattening the method exists to prevent. So the cross-text edges aren't an add-on — they're where the argument is made and where it's guarded.

§3½What the peer review changed (v2, post-IRR179)

A five-model round (IRR179) pressure-tested the nine edges: all five said codeable, with named changes — and one change protects the thesis's central claim.

RHYME is split in two. "Same structure" is not the same as "independent convergence." So RHYME becomes (1) STRUCTURAL-MATCH — the codeable edge — and (2) INDEPENDENT-CONVERGENCE — a downstream verdict that must rule out historical contact and common ancestry. And a new edge does that ruling-out: CITE / ECHO (explicit borrowing) whose whole job is to disqualify a pair from convergence. Augustine quoting Paul is a CITE, not a RHYME. Without this, inherited traditions would be miscounted as independent discoveries — so the change makes the "no historical contact" claim rigorous, not assumed.

The other v2 changes: the cross-text family splits into Convergence (RHYME/COUSIN/ECHO/GENERALIZES) vs Divergence (FALSE-FRIEND/COUNTER/INVERT); new edges ELABORATES · REINTERPRETS · GENERALIZES (the emergent-pattern layer) and IRONIC (a move whose surface ≠ its real operation — the "her reappraisal may be motivated" insight, as an edge); COUNTER fixed (different agents, opposite valence); FALSE-FRIEND loses its "trap/intent" framing; and every edge gets a dual name — an operational one for the schema (FEEDS, FILLS, EXACT-STRUCTURAL-MATCH) and the memorable alias for reading (THEN, ANSWERS, RHYME). §4Across works — the rhyme you asked to see

Take M4 — a mediator asserts an enduring love the beloved doubts — and look for that move elsewhere:

RHYME

"She Loves You" M4 — she loves you (a friend asserts a love you doubt)  ::  Romans / the gospel — God loves you (Paul asserts a love you doubt). Same move, different voice and stakes — and the same residual gap: do you love back? This is the bridge into Romans: the reciprocity gap is shared.

COUSIN

"She Loves You" (a friend counsels you toward reconciliation)  ::  "Hey Jude" (a friend counsels you toward accepting a new love). Same friend-counsels-you-toward-love move — but the outcome differs (repair vs. begin). A cousin, not a rhyme.

FALSE-FRIEND

A possessive "come back to me" ballad uses the same words as reconciliation-counsel but does a different move — control, not the rightly-ordered return. Same surface, opposite operation. Flagged, so it is never counted as convergence.

Do this across the corpus and the edges become a map: the rhymes are the convergences (Q2), the cousins are the lineages, the false-friends are the traps the method is built to catch.

§5Status & what's next

The taxonomy has been through peer review (IRR179, §3½) and is now at v2: the RHYME-split + the CITE edge protect the comparative claim; the cross-text family splits into convergence vs divergence; edges are dual-named. Still ahead: formalize operation as a valency-frame (so "same operation" is codeable, not subjective), compute the edges automatically from the move-codings (they're hand-drawn today), and run the cross-text convergence detection across the corpus — with CITE ruling out contact first, so a match is a real convergence and not an inheritance. Romans 8 is the v2 taxonomy's first real test.
