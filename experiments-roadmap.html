A living list — kept deliberately in prose so the ideation keeps growing. Some of these are built and linked; some are designed; some are still just good ideas we don't want to lose. This is the thinking, out loud, before it becomes a case study.

The transfer matrix — where each capability has earned its place

Every capability earns its place by transferring. Prove it on a neutral control (the Beatles), transfer to scripture (Romans), then to another religion (Islam next). A capability isn't real until it survives the transfer — and each column is a different generality claim, not a harder version of the last.

The columns are orthogonal proofs: control — does it work at all, on a non-theological text? · scripture — does it survive the theological, where the forks are real? · another religion — does it cross traditions (the Q2 question)? · innovation — does it generate NEW knowledge, not just read? · another discipline — does it read a philosophical / psychological voice and place it in empty coordinate territory? · questions-at-large — does it become a companion, any human question and the frames ring it?

The four axes of who-fills-the-gap

The engine varies the frame — the discipline, as five coordinates (epistemic warrant × ontological commitment × inferential operation × telos × stance). But there are three more axes, orthogonal to it, and the cross-product is a map of how meaning-making varies by lens, language, world-position, and mind.

Frame built — eight disciplinary frames; the persona holds across five model families. Now five coordinates: telos (● green, IRR-locked) and stance (candidate) landed S138 — the coordinate is a point in a 5-axis space. The persona white paper.

Language planned — a translation is a set of moves: every translator fills the gaps their language forces. Code "God foreknew" across Mandarin, Korean, Spanish, Italian and translate back — does the move change? If so, meaning is partly the language's doing (pure Bakhtin: languages carry worldviews). Rides the Romans transfer.

Sitz idea — the project's own Sitz concept, made generative: fill the gap from a position in the world, time, and life — a reader steeped in a given country, or in one of Erikson's eight life-stages, or at a temporal crux (medieval, Renaissance). Not a caricature — reasoned from sourced scholarship, with the fake-persona control catching any that slips into costume.

Cognitive style built · XT2 — the axis that produces surprise. A discipline-frame is predictable (a Jungian gives you archetypes); a high-openness mind leaps. Run on "She Loves You" as trait parameters (CARE-tagged, non-clinical): high-openness landed ~1.6 SD past the entire discipline spread (z = +1.7 / +1.6 across two encoders) — genuine, measured surprise. High-neuroticism shifted the content toward threat and loss but stayed within the spread — a different reading, not a more distant one. The metric tells them apart.

Measuring surprise built

Distinctness (are the frames different?) is the judge's question; surprise (did it produce something unexpected?) is the other one. The creativity literature already knows how to score it: novelty is associative distance — how far from the starting point (the divergent-thinking and remote-associates tradition; the "six degrees" intuition made a metric). Here the starting point is the centroid of the eight discipline fills, and the score is surprise × groundedness — far out and still holding. Built and run in XT2 above: it cleanly separated a leap (openness, +1.6 SD out) from a content-shift (neuroticism, in-range), and replicated across two encoders.

The coordinate-driven flywheel built

The biggest build of the week, and the one that makes the whole thing a system: plot the archive into the coordinate space, and the empty cells light up — positions no source yet occupies. Those empty coordinates are the acquisition list; the library and the coordinate-map define each other. First turn proven (the coverage map → coordinate-aware shopping → acquire loop closed), with a live figure_exom atlas recording every mind as a point. The flywheel on the method page.

Real thinkers, not the model's idea of them built

Everything so far conditions the model on a description of a frame. The deeper version, with more runtime, is to ground the persona in a real thinker's actual texts — Howard Thurman, Freud — pulled from the archive, their epistemic-warrant × ontological-commitment × inferential-operation read off their own writing rather than the model's stereotype. The engine could even run at random: pick a thinker from the library, run them against the grammar, see what they fill. That turns the rolodex from "the model's impression of a physicist" into "this specific mind, from its own words." Now proven: the Augustine reversal grounds the frame in his own Latin across three works and 32 years, and detects his real conditional→unconditional turn on election — two-phase (the conviction flipped before the confidence did). The grounding text, not the name, did the work.

The Dear Abby test idea

"She Loves You" is a young-adult relationship problem — and so is an advice-column letter. A real corpus of them exists (the Dear Abby archive, including a whole religion section). Each letter is already a set of moves with conspicuous gaps and an unanswered question. The engine could unpack the moves, surface the silences, and — the real point — generate the questions the letter can't ask itself. This is where the engine's deepest output shows: not answers, but the questions each frame makes askable.

The product: a Talmud of the imaginary idea

The display form, and maybe the point of the whole thing: a reader brings a real problem — a Dear-Abby letter, a passage, their own question — and the engine renders it as a Talmud page. The text sits in the centre, uncollapsed; the frames array around it as commentary, never merging into one voice. The user picks the frames — or sees several from a single tradition, so they meet variations on a Christian reading of the gap they might never have encountered. It's Bakhtin's polyphony laid out in space, and it's a reading practice, not an answer machine.

The recursive move-tree planned

Today the move-tree is the taken path plus a few branches. The real tree grows state by state: at each move, ask the engine for all the ways it could go next, then recurse, until paths reach the end or stop (a precondition fails). Two shapes — open growth (the whole state-space) and fixed endpoints (all the routes from a given start to a given end: two traditions reaching the same place by different paths). The new part is the recursion plus a branch-evaluator — and we'll likely peer-review that design before building it. The move-tree today.

Computing the edges planned

The edges are still hand-drawn. The instrument defines them and we coded them by hand for the song, but nothing yet computes them from the move-codings. Automating that — and running the cross-text convergence detection with the CITE edge ruling out contact first — is where a single reading finally opens onto the whole corpus. Romans is where it pays off. The edge taxonomy (v2).

How we know it's not just us (or just the model)

Two tests belong on the roadmap because showing them is the defence — the method of inquiry, on the record.

The naive baseline (XT4) built — what does a bare LLM do with no instrument? A generic gap-find prompt, run N times, vs the framed fills. The naive prompt clusters (generic); the framed fills spread — and that spread is the value-add. This is the row that answers "isn't this just an LLM?" — no: the instrument is what makes the readings diverge.

The reliability tests built — do independent coders and models agree? IRR177 ran five cold model-coders plus an automated coder against the author's reference on the control text — and the panel corrected the author's own bias. The 5-LLM IRR pattern also reviewed the design before building (IRR178/179) and validated the axis-set (IRR183). This is how a solo researcher gets inter-rater reliability without a committee — and it answers "isn't this just the author?"
