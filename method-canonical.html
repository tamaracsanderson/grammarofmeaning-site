Thesis-grade insights

You asked for two things here: split these by type, and say what "finding" vs "insight" means. Both done.

Finding vs insight, and the two types. An insight is a realisation about how the method works — a design or rigor observation (most of what's below: a control, a caught bug, an honest limit). A finding is a result about the world the method studies — a claim about meaning-making or religion, which must clear the reliability gate before it counts. Almost everything we have right now is the first kind — and that is the honest state of a project at Stage 6 = 0: the instrument and its controls are real; the substantive findings are still candidates, gated. That split is the thesis contribution — the method, made auditable — so it's worth showing plainly.
Which "coding"? — your recurring question, answered once. The project has two different coding operations, and they're at very different stages. Keeping them apart is essential:
CodingWhat it doesWhereStatus
figure-codingplace a figure at a coordinate on the six axes (E·O·M·T·S·G) — the atlasfigure_exom31% (536 / 1,715)
passage-codingcode an individual passage with tradition-internal first-cycle codes — Stage 6passage_codes0% (Stage 6 = 0)
The "coding that isn't done" is passage-coding (Stage 6 = 0); the 6-coordinate figure-coding you guessed is the one that's partly done (31%). And its gap is mostly a coding gap, not a no-source gap (TG-4) — we hold the texts, we haven't coded them; only for a few figures is it secondary-sources-only (the Merleau-Ponty case) or true absence. Semantic indexing is neither — it's how you find a passage (full-text + embeddings search), a retrieval precondition, not a coding step. So: figure-coding places minds, passage-coding reads texts, semantic indexing fetches them.

If it goes in the defense it needs to be a recorded test (a few religious prompts, qwen's refusals logged), not a recollection — otherwise it's exactly the plausible-but-uncited claim the method exists to catch.

Method vs coding — the distinction that scopes the whole problem. There are two very different things you can ask an LLM to do here, and they have opposite reliability profiles:

  • Critique the method (the IRR rounds) — "is this segmentation rule reproducible? is MCA the right embedding?" On this, an LLM ensemble is plausibly stronger than a human panel: broad literature recall, no single-discipline blind spot, five independent angles. This is most of what the IRRs do, and it's where the ensemble is at its best.
  • Code the texts (apply the instrument to real passages) — "what is this figure's ontological-commitment?" This is where the human-baseline gap bites, and it applies to only a handful of rounds. Interpretation of a specific text is exactly where five variants-of-one-learner may share a blind spot.

So the "five LLMs aren't five raters" caveat (TG-1b) is real but scoped: it's a ceiling on the coding, much less on the method critique. The page shouldn't let the caveat swallow the whole ensemble.

Right now, the human coder is the researcher. A solo MDiv student can't fund a human inter-rater panel (≥3 theologians + a KR specialist) — that's outside the cost constraint, honestly stated. So this is marked as a limitation, not papered over.

The honest counter, which belongs in the defense: individual human coders are also trained on overlapping material and arrive with strong priors and gaps — "independent human raters" is itself an idealization. The LLM ensemble trades one correlated-error problem for another; the honest move is to name where the LLM is stronger (scale, cost, breadth, no single-tradition allegiance) and where it may miss (shared training bias, interpretation of a hard text), rather than pretend either panel is clean.

The workarounds — how to get partial human signal within the constraint:

  • One human coder (the researcher), on a bounded set — pre-registered. Code a limited, pre-declared sample (e.g. Romans) by hand, report human–LLM agreement on it as the thesis's reliability probe. Small-N but real, and pre-registration keeps it honest.
  • An external benchmark: compare our seatings against an independent coded corpus — the Database of Religious History (Slingerland) codes ~400 traditions on its own scheme. Agreement with a corpus we didn't build is a validity signal that doesn't cost a panel.
  • Report the disagreement structure, not just an α. We already know the models disagree (that's what the IRRs measure); where they disagree (which axis, which figure-type) is itself data, and the truth-judge cross-model check (TG-6) already turns disagreement into a provenance signal. Lean into that rather than chasing a single number.

Double-click: TG-2 — the Jung case, and why contact is a spectrum, not a gate

Your question: "can we have a rhyme that's a structural match but not independent-convergence — like Jungian analysis, which has some Eastern religion in it?" Yes — exactly. That's the case the split was built for:

  • Jung's individuation and a Buddhist liberation move can be a genuine RHYMES (same operation, different agent/voice) — a real structural match.
  • But Jung read Eastern texts, so the pair also carries a CITES edge (demonstrable contact). That disqualifies it from independent-convergence — while leaving the rhyme itself intact and interesting.

So a pair can be a real rhyme and not independent. The rhyme is still a finding; it's just a finding about influence, not about two traditions arriving somewhere separately.

And your caution is right, and it's now on the page: "I don't want to over-emphasize no-contact — there's often more contact than assumed (trade routes, merchants, colonialism), and requiring 'no contact' is (1) hard to verify and (2) only somewhat interesting." Agreed. So the framing is contact is a spectrum, not a gate: cognate / borrowed / false-cognate / independent, from the comparative method. We score the contact-status and report it with every resonance edge (that's the firewall in A-6) — we do not require "no contact" as a pass/fail. The interesting claim is rarely "these two never touched"; it's "here is the move, here is the contact-status, judge the rhyme accordingly." That's more defensible and more honest than a purity test no historical record could satisfy.

Project principles

The rules that govern what may enter the method at all.

P-1

Place before spawn

locked

Q: When something new shows up — a term, a category, a visualisation — where does it go?

What it is
Before introducing anything, ask which existing layer it belongs to. If it fits one, it goes there. Only if it genuinely fits nowhere is that the rare signal that it may be a new layer — and then it enters by a deliberate lock cycle or an IRR round, never an ad-hoc spawn.
Why it matters
Vocabulary inflation is the failure mode of a method built fast. Every un-placed term is a future conflation. In practice ~99% of new things fit an existing layer.
What's new
Nothing — this is ordinary taxonomic hygiene, stated as a gate so it is enforced rather than intended.
Don't confuse with
A ban on new terms. The rule governs how a term enters, not whether.
P-2

Fit or extend

locked

Q: What happens when a real case doesn't fit any existing category?

What it is
Every taxonomy here is a controlled but open list: a coder must either fit the case to an existing value or extend the list — never silently force a bad fit. An extension earns its place only by having a distinct detection-method; if it shares a test with an existing value it is a relabel, not a new category.
Why it matters
Fixing a category list in advance encodes the conclusion into the instrument. Letting it grow without a test lets it grow forever. The distinct-test requirement is what keeps it finite.
Thinkers
Glaser & Strauss (grounded theory: categories emerge); the requirement of a distinct operational test is our addition.
What's new
The dimensions are fixed; the values emerge. Most coding schemes fix both.
Don't confuse with
Free-form coding. The axes never change mid-run — only the values on them.
P-3

Real text, not remembered text

locked

Q: What does the coder actually read?

What it is
A coordinate must be read off corpus-extracted text whenever a clean source exists. Coding a model's paraphrase from memory is demonstrator-grade only. Four gates admit a source: works by the figure (not about); identity not homonym; chunked not stub; model-readable.
Why it matters
A model's memory reproduces a figure's reputation. Coding John of the Cross's real prose overturned the paraphrase's apophatic label (TG-5). This is the whole argument for building the corpus.
What's new
The gates are a project artifact, forced by real failures (a biography standing in for the author; Eckhart vs Eckhart Tolle).
Don't confuse with
Distrust of models. The model does the reading; the corpus decides what it reads.
Double-click: the four extraction gates, and the case that forced them

Gate 1 — works BY, not works ABOUT. Rejects biographies, commentary, and editorial apparatus standing in for the author. The specimen: a podcast titled "How to Be Awesome at Your Job" surfaced as a candidate stand-in for the Book of Job.

Gate 3 — chunked, not stub. A passage must be real text (500–2,200 chars), not a metadata shell.

Gate 4 — English / model-readable. Honest about what this excludes: it is a live bias-vector, not a neutral filter.

Grammar — the foundation

Everything else in this method is built on one unit and one relation: the move and the edge. Define these two wrongly and every layer above inherits the error. This section defines them, in the order they are needed.

A-1

A move

locked

Q: What is the unit we code? What is the atom?

What it is
A move is one operation and its arguments — what the text does, not what it says.
Why it matters
It is the unit that makes traditions comparable. Two texts sharing no vocabulary, no era, and no contact can still perform the same move; that is the only thing a cross-tradition claim can be made of. Comparing sentences compares languages; comparing moves compares acts.
Thinkers
Fillmore (frame semantics: a verb activates a frame with enumerable slots — the move is a case-grammar of an act). Austin/Searle stand behind "what the text does" but we do not use speech-act taxonomy.
What's new
Applying case-grammar to interpretive acts across traditions, and then coding what each slot leaves empty.
Don't confuse with
The sentence or the line. A move is derived, not the surface string — one sentence can carry two moves, and one move can span a stanza.

A2

A move sits in one state

A-2

state

locked · D172

Q: What does one move hold fixed? Where does time live?

What it is
A state is one move held still: one operation, one fixed agent / substrate / outcome, one narrator, one situation. A text is a sequence of states (M1→M8).
Why it matters
Three consequences follow, and each is load-bearing: (1) time lives in the edges, not the slots — a slot is a still; the motion between stills is an edge; (2) a state-jump is what a SUBSTITUTE does — it voids the premise, so it doesn't complete this state, it starts a different one (B-4); (3) every state has three horizons, because three minds stand at three different times — the agent (inside it), the narrator (telling it), the reader (receiving it, possibly centuries later). Those are the three seers (B-3).
Thinkers
Reiter / the situation calculus — do(a, s), an action a performed in a situation s; s is the state. This is the formalism the move-graph already runs on (via Nazmus Saquib), so the word is not a preference — it is the term the formalism supplies. Genette (focalisation, diegetic levels → the seers).
What's new
Nothing. That is the point: the word was already ours, borrowed, and being ignored.
Don't confuse with
A frame (= a coordinate; a possible mind — D-1). A paradigm (= the world the content assumes — A-7). A state is neither: it is one move, held still.

A 1963 pop song is Western but globally received, secular, and short enough to code completely. It is the control precisely because it is not scripture (see C-4: the song has no interpretive fork, Romans has five — that contrast is what the control is for).

A-4

The three layers

locked

Q: Why are the seven slots not a flat list?

What it is
The slots sit on three layers: H0 narrator — the FRAME layer (who tells it, from what point of view). H1 proposition — the ASSERT layer (operation · agent · substrate · outcome). H2 modifiers — the QUALIFY layer: two riders that sit on H1 slots (type rides operation; voice rides agent).
Why it matters
H1 is what is asserted; H2 is how it is qualified. "Love" is asserted; that it is a report-type move in the active voice are qualifications of that assertion. Keeping them on separate layers is what lets a coder disagree about the qualification while agreeing about the assertion — which is exactly what the reliability data shows happening.
Thinkers
Van Valin (Role-and-Reference Grammar's layered clause — the H0/H1/H2 architecture is borrowed wholesale).
Don't confuse with
Importance. H0 is not "the most important slot" — it is the outermost scope.
A5

A-5

An edge

locked

Q: The grammar codes one move. What codes the relation between moves?

What it is
An edge is a coded relation between two moves. Edges are where time, argument, and comparison live — none of which fit inside a single state.
Why it matters
The slots alone cannot express that Romans 8 builds, or that a later move answers an earlier gap, or that a move here rhymes with a move in another tradition. Every claim the thesis makes is an edge-claim.
Thinkers
SKOS (match-grades: exact / close / related), RST · PDTB · SDRT (discourse relations), Gentner (structure-mapping: correspondence is relational, not surface-similar), Genette (transtextuality), the comparative method in historical linguistics (cognate / borrowed / false-cognate / independent).
Don't confuse with
A slot. A slot is a still; an edge is the motion or the correspondence between stills.

The edge taxonomy

One name per edge (your call — the dual-naming is retired). Seventeen edges in four families, and the four families group into two kinds — the distinction that resolves what "edge" is allowed to mean.

The two kinds — your resolution to the "are they all edges?" conflict. Yes, they are all edges (a coder needs one word for "a coded relation"). But they split into two, and the split is load-bearing:
KindWhat it isFamiliesScope
LINKAGEa fact about one tradition's own logic — how its moves hold together, and how it passes gaps to its own later readersA (coherence) + B (reception)within a text / within a tradition
RESONANCEan analyst's correspondence claim — that a move in one tradition matches one in another. A "rhyme." Never a native connection between the traditions.C (convergence) + D (divergence)across texts / across traditions

A frame is a possible mind: one point on six categorical axes — a mind described by its commitments rather than by its owner's name. It exists whether or not any real thinker occupies it.

The geometry — how you make a map out of words

You marked the old version of this section "gibberish." It was. Here it is in plain English; the technical names are kept, but they come after the idea, not instead of it.

The problem, stated plainly. A figure is not "6.3 on epistemic-warrant." A figure is revelation, or reason. Those are names, not quantities. But to draw a map you need distances — the whole point of a map is that near means similar. So the question the geometry has to answer is uncomfortable: how far is "revelation" from "reason"? There is no ruler. The method is whatever answers that question without inventing a ruler.

What we tried first, and threw away

PCA on one-hot vectors. "One-hot" means turning each category into its own yes/no column: is-revelation = 1, is-reason = 0, and so on. Then run PCA (principal component analysis — the standard workhorse for finding the main directions of variation in numeric data). The problem: PCA treats those 1s and 0s as if they were measurements on a ruler. It will cheerfully compute that the midpoint between "revelation" and "reason" is 0.5, which is not a position any mind holds. Distances that come out of it are arithmetic on labels. Five cohorts rejected it unanimously — including rejecting our own working prototype.

UMAP and t-SNE. These make the prettiest pictures, and they are what most people reach for. Rejected for two reasons: they distort global distance (two clusters far apart on screen may not be far apart in the data — these methods preserve who your neighbours are, not how far away the strangers are), and they are not reproducible (re-run with a different seed, get a different picture). Both are fatal here, because our claim is literally "these two are close." A method that rearranges the room each time cannot support that sentence.

What we use, and why

MCA — Multiple Correspondence Analysis. It is the standard method for exactly our data shape: several categorical variables, many cases, no numbers anywhere. Three properties earn it the job:

  • It is native to nominal data. It never pretends a category is a quantity. Distance is derived from co-occurrence patterns — two values are close if the figures holding them tend to hold the same other things. That is a real answer to "how far is revelation from reason," and it is an answer the data gives rather than one we impose.
  • It corrects for category mass. A rare value is not drowned by a common one — panpsychism (1 figure) still gets to say something next to personal-agentive (232). This is also the property that bit us — see the data-quality catch below.
  • Its axes are interpretable. You can read what a dimension means off which categories load onto it. That is why the results section can name the axes in English instead of calling them "dimension 1."

Cross-checked against Gower + PCoA. A second, independent route: Gower distance is a way of measuring how different two mixed-type records are, computed pairwise and directly; PCoA then lays those distances out in space. It puts the distance metric first, where MCA derives distance from structure. The point of running both: if two methods that work on different principles still agree about who is near whom, the nearness is more likely to be real than an artifact of either.

And we report Greenacre-adjusted inertias, never naive MCA percentages. "Inertia" is MCA's word for how much of the structure this dimension accounts for — the analogue of "variance explained." Raw MCA percentages are known to understate badly for a mechanical reason, and Greenacre's correction is the standard fix. We report the corrected number because the uncorrected one would flatter us in the other direction, and a number that flatters is a number nobody can check.

Post-it — why this section matters more than it looks This is the part a quantitative examiner will go at hardest, and it is the part where the strongest thing we can say is what we rejected: we had a working PCA prototype, the cohorts said it was invalid, and we threw it away. A method that can name the tool it abandoned and why is much harder to attack than one that only ever reports what worked. That's the argument this section should make, and the old version buried it under vocabulary.

Post-it — the answer to "why does it matter that we built a DB, versus just using LLM generation?" This has a cited, locked answer, and it is one line in the provenance index: "paraphrase-coding = demonstrator-grade + confirmation-bias; corpus text confirmed one finding and corrected another."

That is the whole argument, and it is empirical rather than principled: coding Śaṅkara from real text confirmed the apophatic reading (4/4). Coding John of the Cross from real text corrected it — his actual pastoral prose came back personal-agentive 3/4, overturning the paraphrase that had over-stated his apophaticism. You can only run that test if you have the corpus. Without the DB, we would have shipped the reputation and called it a finding — and it would have been a nice finding, exactly like the Augustine pastiche at D-1. That's the case, and I'd make it in one sentence at the defense: the database is not infrastructure, it is the control condition.
