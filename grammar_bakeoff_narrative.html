Method · follow one move through the grammar

Meet a single word: predestined.

Open Paul's letter to the Romans, chapter 8. Among its lines sits one verb — predestined (proōrisen). Most readers slide past it. We're going to stop on it and watch what happens when we ask it to give up its anatomy.

By the end, this one move will have a full skeleton — who acts, on what, toward what — and a set of tags that let it find its rhyme in another tradition. That rhyme is the thing we're learning to make computable.

scroll to follow it ↓
First — the world the move sits in

Before the move, the stage it stands on.

A move never floats free. So before we touch the verb, we fill in the world that produced it — what the codebook calls the sitz: where, when, and how the world was.

"And we know that for those who love God all things work together for good… For those whom he foreknew he also predestined to be conformed to the image of his Son… those whom he predestined he also called, and those whom he called he also justified, and those whom he justified he also glorified." — Romans 8:28–30 (the demonstration seed) · illustrative coding
The world here: Paul, around 57 CE, dictating to mixed Jew–Gentile house-churches in Rome — a city under the long shadow of an imperial cult and a recent edict that had expelled Jews from the city. That backdrop isn't decoration; it stocks what frames are even available to him. We read it through four layers and scan it against ten substrate categories.
→The setting is a wrapper; the move sits inside it. Set the world first, then code the verb.

1

Over the stage, a lens it never argues for.

The move assumes a frame so deep it's never stated — what the codebook calls the paradigm. For predestined, that frame is providence: a world in which God governs the arc of things.

Paul doesn't argue for providence. He reasons from it. The test is simple: what does this move assume that it never argues for? — and is that frame the text's own, or one I'm bringing? Here it's intrinsic (Paul's), not an overlay (mine).
→The paradigm is the lighting over the stage. Change the lighting and the same move means something else.

→A move is a verb with a before and an after. That structure is the same in every tradition — only the words change.
3

Then the move gets tagged.

A skeleton isn't enough to compare two moves. So we tag this one on a handful of shared axes — the qualifiers. Each tag is a controlled value with a yes/no test, so a second coder would reach the same tag.

For predestined, the tags light up like this:
operation-type transformative (contested) voice active mechanism by-grace basis revelation telos union / salvation
voice = active: God does the predestining; the human is the object. mechanism = by-grace: it takes effect not by anything earned but by gift (charis) — and this is exactly the field where the big cross-tradition rhymes live. basis = revelation: Paul grounds it in what was disclosed, not in argument. telos = union: the whole arc aims at being joined to Christ.
→The contested tag is on purpose. Where coders disagree (transformative? constitutive?), we flag it for the typologist — we don't smooth it over.

4

And it doesn't stand alone — it's a link in a chain.

Romans 8 strings five moves into one sequence — the "golden chain." Predestined sits in the middle. Each arrow is a flow edge; what comes after is the move's consequence.

foreknew→ predestined→ called→ justified→ glorified
↑ five moves, one flow. The consequence downstream of the whole chain: assurance — "who shall separate us from the love of Christ?" (Rom 8:31–39).
→A coded move carries its before (sitz), its after (consequence), and its neighbors (the flow). Now it's ready to meet another tradition.
5

A second move appears — and the rhyme fires.

Far from Rome, in a tradition Paul never encountered, sits another move with the same shape: the Pure Land idea of other-power (tariki) — being carried, not by your own effort, but by a power beyond you.

Both are by-grace moves — carried by a power beyond the self — so they link to the same shared value-node. That shared neighbor is the rhyme. But they don't collapse into one thing: predestined aims at union, tariki at liberation. The differences are the finding, not noise. This is "the same move in a different voice" — an analogy you can carry, not a claim that two traditions secretly agree.
→A rhyme is valid only when the structure corresponds — recorded with its differences. Surface similarity doesn't count.
6

Here's the quiet trick: the rhyme is now computable.

Pull back — one move among many.

Now imagine not one move but thousands, each coded this way, each linked through shared neighbors. The biggest, most-connected stars are the moves that rhyme across the most traditions.

Illustrative — sizes, links, and counts are representative of the design, not coded data. Coding is at zero; no passages are coded yet.
Predestined is one move, linked to by-grace — and by-grace turns out to be a big, well-connected star. Sizing every node by how many traditions it rhymes with, the picture becomes the thesis: the most-connected stars are the portable, little-t observations.
→Follow one move all the way in, and you've built one star. Thousands of stars, lit the same way, make the constellation.
