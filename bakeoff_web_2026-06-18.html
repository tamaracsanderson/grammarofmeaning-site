Design rationale
Concept: a night-sky scrollytelling journey. You follow ONE idea — Tengri, the sky-god, who is not yet in the library — as it travels the engine and becomes a bloom in the garden. The idea rides with you as a pinned token whose state changes at every step, so the abstraction stays concrete.
Optimized for: a stranger's "aha" — the page itself performs the pipeline (type an idea, watch the check; watch archives light up in parallel; hover a passage to see it labelled). No stage names, no internal jargon left unglossed.
Differs from a structure-mirror: a factory diagram answers "what are the parts?" — this answers "what happens to MY idea, and can I trust it?" It's a narrative with one protagonist, not a labelled blueprint. The honest nuance (pre-scaffolded ground; logged misses) is dramatized, not footnoted.

What happens when Tengri wants in?

Tengri is the eternal blue sky — the sky-god of the old Turkic and Mongol peoples of the Eurasian steppe.

It isn't in our collection yet. Watch how a single idea gets checked, gathered, read, and finally takes its place among different traditions.

1

The ground is already half-planted.

A common myth: the engine starts from nothing. It doesn't.

Before Tengri ever arrives, a person sketched a hypothesis-list of traditions worth including, and earlier passes already produced background research, reliability reports, and first-draft notes. The library is a partially-built structure — shelves with labels, some already full.
So Tengri doesn't enter a void. It enters a room with a map on the wall — and lands either in an empty shelf that was waiting for it, or beside something already there.
2

First question: is it already here?

Every new idea is checked against what the library already holds. Try it yourself.

Tengri Four Noble Truths ubuntu apophatic prayer
The honest part: a "miss" is good news — it's exactly the case the engine exists for. A "hit" just means we route you to what's already grown.
3

Not in the library. So we hunt — everywhere at once.

Here's the principle that matters most: Tengri is searched everywhere, not just in its home region. Steppe archives and Western ones fire in parallel.

Searching…
Why this matters (the "parity" rule): an archive is searched for what it contains, never for whose region it "belongs to." A Mongolian manuscript library and a German one are both asked about Tengri. Skipping the "obvious" ones is how blind spots form.
4

Everything found is stamped — and so is everything missed.

Each text comes in carrying a receipt: where it came from, how it was found.

⚑ The honest bias-trail
When an archive comes up empty, that's recorded too — not silently dropped. Over time these logged absences become a visible map of where the world's record is thin. The gaps are part of the finding, not hidden under the rug.
✓ found · Mongolian manuscript archive ✓ found · Eurasian oral-history collection ✗ empty · major Western theology index — logged
Trust comes from the receipts. A scholar can later ask, "what did you look at, and what came back nothing?" — and get an answer.
5

Big texts become small, quotable pieces.

A whole book is too big to compare. So each text is broken into passages — the smallest unit you could quote and stand behind.

"By the will of Möngke Tengri — the Eternal Sky — under whose power all things live, this decree is given."
↑ one passage, lifted from a 13th-century steppe edict. It can now be pointed at, quoted, and labelled on its own.
Container vs. atom: the book is the container; the passage is the atom we actually analyze. A small collection of books can yield a vast number of passages.
6

Labelled in Tengri's own vocabulary first.

The rule that protects against bias: no outside category is forced onto the passage. It's described first in its own tradition's words — by a small panel of AI reviewers each playing a different role.

Insider
"This is reverence for the sky as the source of legitimate rule."
Möngke-Tengrisky-mandate
Outsider
"A high-god sanctioning earthly authority — recognizable cross-culturally."
divine-sanction
Practitioner
"You invoke Tengri before acting; the sky is witness, not idol."
invocationwitness-sky
Notice: nobody wrote "monotheism" or "God." Those are our categories — imported later, carefully, only when the passage's own words have been heard.
Why three voices? One reviewer can be confidently wrong. Three independent readings, with their agreement scored, stand in for a human rating team the solo researcher can't afford.
7

Later, the surprise: where Tengri rhymes.

Only after many traditions are labelled this way does the engine look across them — for ideas that arose independently yet echo each other. We call it a rhyme: different traditions, making the same move.

☼ Tengri (steppe)
the open sky as impartial witness to every act
☸ a contemplative line
"sky-like mind" — awareness as boundless, unstained space
No contact. No borrowing. Two peoples reached for the sky to say something about an open, watching awareness.
This is the payoff. Convergence between traditions across traditions is interesting because most things don't converge — the rhyme is the rare signal worth studying.

The library isn't a list. It's a sky-map of many traditions' observations — each a bloom, with glowing lines drawn between the ones that rhyme.
