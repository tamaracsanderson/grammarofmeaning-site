Two case studies in which the method was peer-reviewed by five independent AI models before being built, and the reviews caught real, load-bearing problems.

The method reviews itself

Before building an instrument, we hand its design to five independent models and ask them to break it. Twice this session it worked exactly as intended — the reviews caught real problems, and one of them protected the thesis's central claim.

§1Why review a design before building it

A solo researcher has no committee in the room. So the project substitutes a five-model panel (GPT, Gemini, Claude, DeepSeek, Solar) — a discipline borrowed from the project's own reliability method. The rule is simple: validate the design before you build it. A one-day review round is far cheaper than a week spent building the wrong thing. Below are the two rounds from this session; both returned "proceed, with named changes," and both changed the build.

§2IRR178 — reviewing the generative engine's design

5/5 · proceed with named changes — do not build as drawn

We proposed a "persona rolodex" — filling an interpretive gap from many disciplinary frames — and asked the panel to critique the *design*, not approve it. They agreed it was measuring the wrong thing:

  • Wrong construct. We were measuring divergence; divergence isn't creativity (random noise diverges too). The right object is quality-diversity — an archive of grounded, non-redundant candidates across a defined space (from evolutionary computation).
  • Confounded mechanism. A prompt-persona mixes the frame, the named-person stereotype, the wording, and the model's own priors. Without controls, "the physicist" measures the model's stereotype of a physicist.
  • Biased instrument. Embedding "distance" measures distance-from-training-data, not conceptual distance — and an LLM can't judge its own bias.
Every one of these reshaped the build: we replaced the persona names with three coordinates (epistemic warrant × ontological commitment × inferential operation), added a fake-persona control, and re-framed the whole experiment around quality-diversity. The result — the persona white paper — is a far better instrument than the one we'd have built without the round.

§3IRR179 — reviewing the edge taxonomy

5/5 · codeable with named changes — and one change protects the thesis

We proposed nine named "edges" — the connecting lines between moves, including the cross-text ones where convergence lives. The panel converged, from five different angles, on a single load-bearing correction:

"Same structure" is not the same as "independent convergence." A move here and a move there can share an identical shape and still not be independent — because of borrowing, common ancestry, or a shared source. So the convergence edge had to split: the structural match (codeable) versus independent convergence (a later verdict that must rule out contact). And a new edge — CITE — exists precisely to catch the borrowing: Augustine quoting Paul is a citation, not an independent discovery.

The project's defining question is "convergence across traditions that share no historical contact." Before the round, the taxonomy would have counted inherited traditions as independent discoveries — quietly inflating the central claim. After it, that claim is rigorous rather than assumed. The review didn't weaken the thesis; it hardened it. (Full taxonomy: edges & meta-move.)

§4What this demonstrates

The point of publishing these rounds is the point of the whole project: the method is open, and it is auditable. The critiques are on the record — including the ones that said we were wrong — and so is what we changed in response. A design that survives five independent adversarial reviews, with its revisions documented, is a stronger object than one that was simply asserted. And the same five-model machine that validates a design is the one that, elsewhere in the lab, measures whether an instrument is reliable — one apparatus, two jobs.
