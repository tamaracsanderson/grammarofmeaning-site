How the grammar was made

A companion to the grammar itself: that page shows what the grammar is. This one shows how it was arrived at.

The grammar wasn't declared. It was arrived at — and the surprising part is from where.

Four separate paths reached the same six-part skeleton without coordinating. An intuition I'd carried for years — that meaning gets made in the between, and that the practical mechanics of that deserve as much attention as the beliefs. A systems-thinking sketch — a move has a substrate, an operation, and an outcome, like a stock-and-flow drawn as a graph. Four scholars, read fresh this month. And a close reading of a single scripture. None was looking at the others. They landed in the same place.

That agreement is the grammar's own evidence. It isn't arbitrary, it isn't an artifact of one source, and it isn't something an AI handed me. It's where independent paths meet — the same kind of evidence the project looks for in the traditions themselves: when paths that developed apart arrive at the same structure, the structure is worth taking seriously.

It is also not finished. What follows is a working draft, caught mid-process on purpose.

A little more on the three that mattered most

The four scholars came third, not first.

Reinhart Koselleck (concepts carry time inside them), Arthur Lovejoy (ideas travel and recombine across centuries), Charles Taylor (we don't just label the world, we constitute it in language), Ann Taves (the smallest religious move is someone deeming something special). They didn't author the skeleton — they arrived to test and sharpen one that intuition and systems-thinking already had. Remove the scholars and the skeleton still stands; remove the intuition and there's nothing to stand.

The method was built to catch its own bias.

The five-model panel was set up to argue with us, and it did — it named a flattening we couldn't see from inside. Publishing that, rather than hiding it, is the point: a method you can audit is worth more than one that only shows its wins.

Paul was field-tested in his own world.

Before asking whether a move in Romans rhymes with a move in another tradition, we reconstructed Paul's setting — Rome around 57 CE, the mixed Jewish-and-Gentile house-churches, the aftershock of an imperial expulsion order, the politics of the grain supply. The reason is discipline: a rhyme only counts if it's a real structural echo, not an accident of two traditions sharing the same circumstances.

And the interpreter has a setting too — we watched how Augustine's own world, centuries later, colors the way he reads Paul.

Where it stands

A working draft — provisional on purpose. It has survived a scripture it didn't choose and twelve other passages, and it was reached independently from four directions. It has not passed the gate that would let us call it settled.

The gate ahead

Fourteen held-out passages, frozen in advance — and an outside human expert, a typologist working beyond the Western language family. Until it clears that, we'll keep calling it a draft.
