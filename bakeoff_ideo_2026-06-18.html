Design rationale

Concept: a knock at the door. We follow ONE idea — Tengri, the Turkic-Mongol sky-god — as a character who arrives uninvited and watches herself get welcomed into a library that's already half-built. The engine's steps become beats in a guest's arrival story.

The one thing I optimized for: a single felt takeaway — "a stranger gets the same welcome the regulars got." That one feeling (fairness / hospitality) is what every step is in service of.

How it differs from a structure-mirroring diagram: no stage names as headlines, no pipe-and-station rail, no workstream badges. The architecture is still all here — it's just demoted to a quiet "what the machine actually does" margin note beside each story beat. The story leads; the system follows.

Beat 1 · The knock

Someone the library wasn't looking for

The protagonist arrives.

Most ideas that reach this library were already on the guest list. The Buddha's Four Noble Truths, for instance, walked in long ago — its texts shelved, its terms learned, its passages quoted and cross-referenced. It belongs.

Then there's Tengri — the eternal blue sky worshipped across the Turkic and Mongol steppe for two thousand years. Tengri is not on most guest lists. No tidy canon. No single holy book. A tradition carried in oaths, in open-air ritual, in the title of khans who ruled "by the will of Tengri." When she knocks, the honest question is: is she even in here yet?

I am older than most of your books, and yet I am not sure you have a shelf for me. I have come to find out.
This is the story of what happens when an idea the library didn't plan for shows up at the door.
Beat 2 · The half-built house

The room was already partly furnished

The honest nuance: this is not a search starting from zero.

Here's the part that surprises Tengri. The library is not empty, and it is not blank toward her. Before she ever arrived, someone had sketched a plan of which traditions ought to be represented — and Tengrism was on that hopeful sketch. Background research reports had been filed. Earlier notes, drafted by AI assistants, had laid down a first rough outline of what a Tengri shelf might hold.

So she doesn't walk into a void. She walks into a room with her name already pencilled on an empty shelf — scaffolding waiting to be filled, or corrected, by the real thing.

What this means plainly: the engine starts from a hypothesis-driven list of traditions to include, plus prior research write-ups and AI-drafted groundwork. A new idea is checked against an existing structure — it's "add to a partly-built library," never "build from nothing."
what the machine actually does
The arrival is logged as a want — a single row in a standing wish-list (the cart). Nothing here depends on anyone remembering it later; the moment a gap is noticed, it's written down.
→ shopping_list row · status: pending
Ah. You were expecting me. Sort of.
Beat 3 · The errand

They look for her everywhere at once

The most important fairness move in the whole engine.

Now the library goes hunting for Tengri's texts. And here is the move that matters most: it does not only look where you'd expect. It doesn't quietly decide "Tengrism is Central Asian, so check the Central Asian shelf and call it done."

Instead, the same request fans out to many archives at the same time — Mongolian and Turkic collections, yes, but also the great Western digital libraries, African scholarly journals, Latin American and Japanese ones. A study of Tengri written in Brazil is asked for with the exact same priority as one written in Ulaanbaatar.

The project calls this the Parity Principle: an archive is chosen for what it contains, never for whose scholarship it is. A tradition is searched for everywhere — so the library doesn't accidentally treat "non-Western" ideas as second-class guests.
A stranger gets the same errand run that the regulars got.
Beat 4 · The honest haul

What came back — and what didn't

The misses are kept, not hidden.

Texts come home: a few primary sources, scholarly studies, ethnographies of steppe ritual. But Tengri is a tradition of spoken faith, so some shelves come back thin. A normal library would quietly shrug and move on.

This one keeps a receipt for every door it knocked on — including the ones that didn't open. The gaps aren't erased; they're written into the record as honest data: here is where Tengri is well-documented, and here is exactly where she isn't.

The provenance ledger — every source, every miss
Mongolian ritual-text collection✓ found
Ethnography of steppe sky-worship (academic)✓ found
Turkic oral-history archive✓ found
Western canonical-scripture index✗ no canon to match
Peer-reviewed English-language monographs✗ sparse — logged as a gap
The misses are the bias trail. Where coverage is thin, that thinness becomes visible — never a silent filter pretending the tradition simply isn't there.

Everything found enters through a single door, and nothing gets in without saying where it came from. If a source ever needs to be questioned later, you can trace it all the way back to the archive it walked in from.

what the machine actually does
Found texts are ingested with full provenance (origin, how-harvested, who-recorded) through one enforced gateway; the source-tried log — hits and misses — is preserved as a corpus-skew audit. Care note: Indigenous and oral material is always included and tagged for community-stewardship review — never deferred or left out.
→ source rows + per-source provenance + source-tried log
Beat 5 · Unpacking the trunk

From whole texts to quotable pieces

A container becomes its contents.

The texts arrive like packed trunks. A trunk is useful, but you can't quote a trunk. So each text is carefully unpacked into its smallest meaningful pieces — a single invocation, one line of an oath, a sentence of an elder's account. Each little piece gets its own address, so it can be pointed to, quoted, and studied on its own.

This is why a library of a few hundred thousand texts becomes a library of millions of passages: one trunk holds many quotable things.

what the machine actually does
Sources are containers; passages are the unit of analysis. Chunking turns ~390k sources into ~9.5M individually addressable, quotable passages — each traceable to its exact spot in the original.
→ passage (anchor) rows, each location-stamped
Beat 6 · Heard in her own language

Described in her words, not borrowed ones

The discipline at the heart of the project.

Now comes the moment Tengri most feared: being described. Will the librarians reach for familiar, foreign categories — file her under "monotheism," shrug her into a Western frame, flatten the blue sky into someone else's god?

No. Each passage is first described in the tradition's own vocabulary — Tengri, Köke Möngke Tengri ("the Eternal Blue Sky"), khan, the steppe oath — before any outside label is allowed near it. And it isn't one librarian deciding alone. A small panel reads each piece from three different chairs:

🏠
The insider

Reads as someone within the tradition would — on its own terms.

🔭
The outsider

The comparativist who sees how it sits among other traditions.

🙏
The practitioner

The one who asks what it's for — what it does in a life.

Why three readers? A solo researcher can't convene a room of human raters, so a small panel of AI reviewers plays insider / outsider / practitioner, and their agreement is measured. Describing a tradition in its own words first — before any imported category — is the project's core rule against bending strangers into shapes that aren't theirs.
They wrote down what I call myself, before they wrote down what I remind them of. That is the whole difference.
Beat 7 · The unexpected echo

She discovers she rhymes with strangers

The payoff — convergence across different traditions.

Once Tengri is fully herself on the shelf, something quiet and remarkable happens. The library, looking across all its traditions at once, notices an echo: the steppe's reverence for the vast, impartial sky rhymes with how other faiths — that never once touched the Mongol world — reached for the same vault of heaven to name what is boundless and just.

Not the same words. Not borrowed from one another. The same move, arrived at independently — like two distant poets stumbling on the same rhyme. That's the finding the whole engine exists to surface.

what the machine actually does
Across coded passages from many traditions, the engine surfaces cross-tradition rhymes — independently-arising parallels across traditions. Convergence, not influence, is the signal worth keeping.
Beat 8 · A place in the garden

The stranger becomes a neighbor

The landing.

The shelf that had only Tengri's name pencilled on it is now full — real texts, in her own words, with an honest note about what's still missing, and a fresh thread of light connecting her to traditions she'll never meet. She isn't a curiosity in a side cabinet. She's planted in the same garden as everyone else, with the same care the Four Noble Truths received.

I came to ask if you had a shelf for me. You did better — you found the threads I share with other traditions.

What this engine really is

A hospitality machine for ideas.

It welcomes a tradition the world half-forgot, describes it in its own words, keeps an honest record of what it couldn't find — and then shows that tradition it was never as alone as it thought. The Four Noble Truths got that welcome. So did Tengri. So does the next stranger at the door.

The system is the same one in the engineers' diagrams — gather, ingest with provenance, chunk, code in-vivo, surface convergence. What changes here is only the question we lead with: not "what are the stages?" but "what is it like to be the idea moving through them?"
