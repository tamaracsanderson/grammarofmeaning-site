Before the traveller arrives

The ground is already built

The library does not start empty. A hypothesis-driven map of traditions was sketched in advance, and earlier research — deep-dive reports, cross-checks between several AI readers, first-pass draft entries — laid the soil. The Four Noble Truths is already planted here; it grew long ago.

So when a new idea shows up, it isn't dropped into a void — it's checked against a structure that already exists.

In short: a scaffolded shelf with marked gaps, not a blank page.
Question 1 · who's knocking?

A new idea asks to come in

Tengri is now a name at the door

Tengri — the eternal blue sky worshipped across the Turkic and Mongol steppe — arrives at the door. Someone names the gap: "We should have this. Do we?"

The request is written down the instant it's noticed — never kept in someone's head.

A want, captured. It joins a standing wish-list of things the library knows it's missing.
Question 2 · do we have you already?

Check the shelves first

Tengri is now not in the library — yet

The engine looks: is Tengri already in the collection? No — the steppe sky-religion is exactly the kind of tradition that's easy to under-collect. That "no" is the interesting moment: the journey to bring it in actually begins.

↺ Contrast — the Four Noble Truths: here the answer is "yes, already held." Its road ended at this door long ago. Tengri's is just starting — which is why we follow Tengri.
Question 3 · where do we look?

Search everywhere at once — not just its home region

Tengri is now being hunted, everywhere at once

The hunt fires across many archives in parallel, on one rule: an archive is asked for what it contains, never for whose scholarship it is. A Mongolian study and a Cambridge study of the sky-god are asked with the same priority.

Question 4 · how do we shelve it honestly?

Bring it in with a paper trail — even the misses

Tengri is now gathered, with its paper trail

Each source enters with a label saying exactly where it came from. And the empty-handed searches are written down too — "looked here, found nothing." That record is the library's honesty: where coverage is thin, you can see it, instead of it hiding as a silent gap.

Every later judgment about Tengri can be traced back to the exact source it came from. where-from · how-fetched · who-wrote-it
Question 5 · what are the quotable pieces?

Break each text into small, quotable units

Tengri is now broken into quotable passages

A whole book is too big to compare. So each text about Tengri — an invocation, a prayer to the eternal blue sky, an ethnographer's field note — is cut into small passages, each one short enough to quote and point to precisely.

One book becomes many passages — the pieces are what actually get studied, not the book as a lump.

Question 6 · in whose words?

Label each piece in Tengri's own vocabulary first

Tengri is now described in its own words

Crucially, the engine does not stamp borrowed categories on it. It labels each passage in the tradition's own terms — Tengri (the sky itself), Köke Möngke Tengri ("eternal blue sky"), the mandate a ruler holds from heaven.

And it isn't one reader deciding alone. A small panel reads each passage from three different chairs, so no single viewpoint sorts it:

the insiderreads as a member of the tradition — does this honor how it understands itself?
the outsiderreads as a comparativist — what does it look like from outside?
the practitionerreads as one who lives it — what does it ask of a life?

Only the tradition's own language goes on first. Comparison comes later — never before.

The payoff · where does it rhyme?

Find where strangers said the same thing

Tengri is now a bloom in the garden

Now the comparison the whole journey was for. Across traditions that never met, the engine surfaces rhymes — independently-arising ideas that line up — and tests whether they hold.

Illustrative example
Illustrative, not a result. The Tengri ↔ Mandate-of-Heaven ↔ sky-God's-covenant rhyme is shown to explain what kind of pattern the engine looks for — sky-powers that legitimate or judge a people, in traditions that never met. It's a candidate of that shape, not a locked finding: real rhymes are coded bottom-up in each tradition's own words first, then tested for whether they actually hold.

That's the question the library exists to make answerable: not "everyone agrees," but where wholly separate peoples reached for the same shape of meaning — and where they didn't.

One honest caveat the journey rests on The engine is not a magic box that creates knowledge from nothing. Tengri landed in ground that was already prepared — a hypothesis-shaped list of traditions, prior research, and earlier draft entries. The engine's real work is to grow that ground honestly and at scale: searching everywhere, logging the misses, listening in each tradition's own words before it ever compares them.
